More videos than ever



First of all, you need to split the traffic. Traffic can be either outgoing (the subscriber downloads data to the network) or incoming (the subscriber downloads data from the network or watches video from the network, if we talk about video) regarding the user. But even when the user watches, ‚Äúrequests‚Äù are created for viewing this or that content and answers about successful delivery, which also occupy some outgoing bandwidth.



It should be noted that video transmitted to users, due to the encoding process, consumes much less traffic than RAW files that users upload to the network for their subsequent processing by codecs and streaming. It's amazing how effective the coding used by Netflix is. For example, a two-hour movie consumed only 459 MB of incoming traffic, while on iTunes the same movie is offered for download with various bitrate options - 4.6 GB for 1080, 4.15 GB for 720, and 1.86 GB for SD, and rival Amazon Prime has the following encoding options - from 579 MB in 1 hour 29 minutes to 1.5 GB in 2 hours 7 minutes, which is significantly inferior to Netflix.







Thus, if Netflix did not encode the video as well as it does, the share of Internet traffic generated by this resource would grow by 2-3 times, with the same traffic. It is also worth noting that many mobile operators offer "unlimited" access to online video services when viewed in low resolutions, which saves traffic not only for Netflix, but also for other services.
