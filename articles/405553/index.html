Historian Henry Adams spoke metaphorically, and not in medical terms, when he described power as "a kind of tumor that kills the sympathies of the victim." But this is not so far from the facts disclosed by Decher Keltner, a professor of psychology at the University of California at Berkeley, after many years of laboratory and field experiments. The subjects, endowed with power, as he discovered in two decades of research, behave as if their brains are injured - they become more impulsive, less aware of the risks, and, most importantly, they are worse at evaluating events from the point of view of other people.

Mirroring is a more subtle version of mimicry, completely happening in the head without our participation.
