The condition of the films was very different, the color films were preserved much better than black and white. But, in any case, the degradation was evident - fading, some films began to peel.



Initially, I planned to pay for the digitization of the film and thus resolve the issue, but when I studied the market, I realized that it would be a failure option. I had about 120 minutes of films (8 reels for about 15 minutes). On average, this is 10 tr. and not very dependent on the city. For a family archive, it seems acceptable, memory is more expensive, but when specifying details it turned out that there was no more specialized equipment, or it was not. Self-made decisions, even with statements on the site about frame-by-frame shooting (more about the technologies below), were made on a video camera, which, given the current state of some films, was too presumptuous in terms of the final quality.



There were 3 main methods of digitization:



  1. Shooting a camcorder from the screen. Even with the ideal quality of the source material, a very degraded copy is obtained.
  2. Lens in the lens. Basically, they offer to shoot again with a video camera, they call it time-lapse shooting, but in fact using the program (which, by the way, is in the public domain) throw out repeated frames and their change. The quality is better, but since editing is poor, the original state should be appropriate.
  3. Honest time-lapse shooting. Since the speed in this process has no effect, it allows you to shoot in RAW formats. Commercially, this option is now for some reason no one offers (or I did not find it).


As you can see from the first photo, I decided to go 3 ways.



The final version of the algorithm is very simple:



- the shooting team is transferred to the camera;

- the camera takes a picture and throws it off on the PC;

- the film is rolled on one frame;

- the cycle repeats.



In my implementation there are small nuances, but about them later.

Everything is based on the Rus projector, which is popular for digitizing. The main advantage is the availability and the ability to choose a drum of two film formats 8mm and "8 super." In the second, the perforation is reduced and due to this the frame area is increased by more than 30%. In addition to this, I just had a projector.



From the improvements:



- cleaned obturator;

- the motor has been replaced, since the standard one cannot be PWM regulated at low speeds;

- the lamp was replaced with a 9-segment LED matrix (on top of it secured a sheet of paper for uniform illumination). LED heatsink picked up from a very old, buggy video card.









The camera chosen is far from the most optimal, but it was the cheapest option that I could find. Canon 1100D, younger model, able to fully work with Canon SDK. The guaranteed resource of its shutter is more than 100,000 frames, I have about as many frames as I had, and the camera went almost new. Of course, a camera without a mirror would be much better here, but it would have cost significantly more, and the whole project would still be cheaper than the cost of commercial digitization.

On software, everything is simple, it took no more than an hour with regard to the search. For the Canon SDK, there is an excellent EDSDKLib shell, added a virtual COM port to its work. The command to make a frame - there, back we are waiting for the command about successful rewinding. The only caveat that for some reason the camera sometimes remained in the mode of resetting files to the PC was decided by adding a delay after shooting.



The program for Atmega is written in AVR Studio, it gives out PWM to the rewind engine until the mechanical limit switch works. According to the mind, it had to be made optical, since it lasts for 40,000 maximum actuations, but the process was already underway and I did not redo it.
