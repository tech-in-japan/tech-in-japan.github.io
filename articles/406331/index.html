  • The song must have the potential. It should not be popular here and now, but become so in the process of rotation.

These are not ‚Äúreinforced concrete‚Äù criteria, but they are trying to be observed at all stations. After the tracks are selected, they are divided into categories. Each station has its own approach.

Not only entertainment, but also an area for research



Scientists study sound from many different angles. Including how it affects productivity. Music can increase productivity, if it is associated with monotonous activities, helps to better concentrate on what is happening and raise the emotional background.



Other scientists are studying the connection between music and physiology, such as goosebumps. This phenomenon even has a name - free-music, and music can cause it. From 55 to 86 percent of people are subject to such effect (examples of scientific works: 1 and 2 ). But most often it happens to those who are open to new experiences and have a rich imagination.



Neuroscientist Daniel Levitin believes that music is a powerful tool for building social connections. In his book, he wrote that this is the only thing that can make a person join the twenty thousandth crowd.



Music directly affects the emotional background: it produces the ‚Äúhappy hormone‚Äù dopamine, increases the level of serotonin and oxytocin, and activates neuroreceptors that are susceptible to other hormones.



Biologists study how animals perceive sound . Many of them have no ears, but they still hear. For example, a grasshopper has special membranes on its front legs.



Music for animals does not sound like for humans. Your cat will not distinguish, Dorn you turned on or Black Sabbath - for her both that and the other will be a set of sounds and noises, not always pleasant. Some of them are not heard by a person.



However, composer David Teie recorded music for cats . He says these are relaxing compositions. It‚Äôs not clear to the end whether cats like it or not, but they definitely react.


Software engineer and musician Andrew Duncan made out music from a combinatorial point of view. He counted the number of variations of the 12-ton chromatic scale. To this end, he combined notes in a circle of 12 elements (semitones). If a particular semitone was present in the selected key, interval, or chord, it was colored white. Otherwise, black. Duncan received 2 ^ 12 = 4096 combinations that make up the music.
