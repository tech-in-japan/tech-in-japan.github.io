The 1840 Babbage Steam Computer or Differential Machine

Somewhere in the 1800s, Charles Babbage invented the first computer, then the word "computer" had a different meaning, and he called his invention the Difference Machine or the Analytical Machine. The ingenious inventor was ahead of his time, but, unfortunately, did not complete his invention, and only a hundred years later the first real computer was invented, but this is another story. And today's article is about the Babbage Analytical Machine.

1. Warehouse - hard disk, memory; 2. Mill - processor; 3. Steam engine - power supply; 4. Printer - printer; 5. Cards of operations - programs; 6. Variable maps - addressing system; 7. Numeric cards - for entering numbers; 8. Control drums - firmware.



Self-counting machine



In this article we will try to find out the device of the Analytical Machine, but first we should note that it belonged to the family of ‚Äúautomatic‚Äù (self-) mechanisms that was widespread since the 1740s.

The centrifugal regulator is the first of the "self-acting" mechanisms of the industrial age. By the way, he is one of the most recognizable parts of the steam engine.





When the engine accelerates, the balls deviate from the axis under the influence of centrifugal force, because of this, the clutch moves and limits the flow of steam, and the car slows down. Slowing the machine lowers the balls and this opens the valve - the steam flow opens, the cycle is closed.



The design of the Difference Machine itself was similar to adding machines, and, like adding machines, the Machine consisted of a long train of gears that add up the numbers and then give out the sum.







Somewhere in 1834, Babbage improved the design, and by returning the amount back to the machine, more complex calculations became available.



The work of the Analytical machine was based on the ‚Äúdevouring of its tail‚Äù, and the system worked thanks to a complex chain of gears that were controlled by punched cards and drums, calculating sums and sending the results to the warehouse, which consisted of a number of gears.



Approximately everything interacted as follows:







  1. Cards of operations (A) indicate to cards of variables (B) that it is necessary to request numbers for calculations;
  2. The numbers are entered from the number cards (C) or from the warehouse (D) and in turn are fed to the input axis (E);
  3. The input axis transfers the numbers to the center wheels (F);
  4. The operation map gives the command to add numbers or multiply or otherwise, and the reels (G) rotate to the position in which their pins correspond to the operation.
  5. The drums activate the levers by connecting the gears of the mill (H) to the central wheels. And already in the mill, certain devices are responsible for addition, multiplication, and other actions;
  6. Gears perform multiplication of the original numbers;
  7. The mill, if necessary, can loop the action, transferring commands to different parts of the punch card;
  8. The result falls on the output axis (I).
  9. The output axis transfers data to the printer (D) or sends it to the warehouse according to variable cards;
  10. Operation cards issue a command to make a call (J) and to stop the Machine. Everything!






Memory: warehouse





Any computer, steam or electronic, requires the ability to store data. In Babbage's invention, he was called a warehouse, and, like almost the entire machine, it consisted of gear wheels arranged in tall columns. On each of the columns only one number was stored that was no longer than fifty digits, and the upper wheel determined the number positively or negatively.



According to my estimates, it will take quite a while before these restrictions cease to satisfy the needs of science.

Charles Babbage

In Babbage's drawings, the warehouse consisted of two parallel rows of high numeric columns, and each one contained one number. One side of the warehouse communicated with the mill.

Programs



There were no programs at that time, or rather, they were already invented, but then they were called operation cards and looked something like this:





Operations Map



The programs were handled by Ada Lovelace, and, like true aristocrats, they gave orders to the drums and variable maps without contacting the workers. Even simple addition involved many parts, and with the help of a large drum one lever could set any value for eighty other levers.



According to the holes in the cards, the drum turns to the levers in different sections, which contain a specific cipher and use different sets of levers.







And although the drums remind roller bars, they act differently. Instead of continuous rotation, the drum rotates to a certain position and then moves forward, pushing and activating a set of necessary levers.

Punch cards



The first system, built on punch cards, was a jacquard loom, and it was he who was inspired by Babbage.





Map of Jacquard, 1850



The principle of their operation is simple and ingenious at the same time: the lever holding the punch cards lowers, pressing the card against a set of spring-loaded horizontal pins. If there is no hole under the pin, then the card shifts the pin and tilts the rod with the hook so that it clings to the pin. Then the pins move up along with the hooks hooked to them.







Logic and cycles



Punch cards and gears are great, but they don‚Äôt make the Difference Machine a computer. From the device for calculating the decimal arithmetic Machine turns into a computer thanks to a small detail - the conventional lever.







This lever is automatically lowered if the result of the calculations requires further action by the program. And if the pin is at a certain position of the drum, and then the lever is lowered, a new cycle of calculations is launched.



Thus, the conventional lever closes the cycle, and the Machine ‚Äúeats its own tail‚Äù: the punched cards control the drums, the drums by the Machine, the Machine by the drums, and the drums by the punched cards.
