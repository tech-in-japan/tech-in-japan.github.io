Programming language is a technological product, but not so simple



Some time ago I had the opportunity to read an interesting book, The Innovator‚Äôs Dilemma . There, with examples of high-tech products, it is shown how new products emerge that first lose to the decisions that currently dominate the market, and then radically change the state of the market.

Another example from our professional field is the development of personal computers. Which initially were not able to compete with the "real" computers of the mid-late 1970s. The authors of the book declare that events in all such cases develop according to the same scenario:







  • First, there is a need for some product that solves a particular problem. Let's say a flexible magnetic disk;
  • since at the initial stage all competing solutions (of which, in principle, are few) are based on the same technologies, the market is divided between more or less similar products;
  • manufacturers of successful products begin to compete with each other and in this competition bring their products almost to the most favorable price-quality ratio. And, most importantly, while existing products are as consistent as possible with the views on how the tasks for which these products are intended to be addressed. Roughly speaking, everyone knows that 8 "floppy disks are excellent for transferring information from one mini-computer to another using flexible magnetic disks. And no one else can imagine another way in the mainstream;
  • somewhere on the sidelines, based on some new discoveries / materials / technologies, a new product appears that is significantly inferior to existing products in almost all respects. Generally more expensive. As a rule, not comparable in capabilities / capacities. But it has a very important quality: it can be used where it is impossible or difficult to apply mainstream solutions. For example, you cannot plug an 8 "drive into a personal computer. And PC owners are not interested in carrying 8" floppy disks. It is just not practical. Whereas a 5.25 "diskette is quite a thing for itself. And it doesn‚Äôt give a damn that at the cost of a kilobyte 5.25" diskette was originally more expensive than 8. "There was still no choice;
  • A new market was formed around the new product, which was initially not at all interesting for mainstream product manufacturers. But, since it was a new market, new players flocked to it, which created serious competition for each other. And this competition quickly translated marginal and ineffective technology into extremely effective. Which soon ceased to be marginal, because as soon as it exceeded the old mainstream products in terms of its totality, it quickly replaced them in their own niche. Roughly speaking, if there are hundreds of thousands of 5.25 "drives and millions of 5.25" floppy disks around, then what's the point of holding onto the much more marginal 8 "devices at the moment?

But not everything is so simple with programming languages



In the scheme of penetration of new technological products into the market described in the Innovator‚Äôs Dilemma, one of the most important reasons for users to switch from old mainstream products to new ones is either simply a significant reduction in the cost of ownership or the acquisition of previously available opportunities + a decrease in the cost of ownership.
