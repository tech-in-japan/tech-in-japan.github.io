Interview programs: creation history, job analysis, development prospects

For thousands of years of evolution, humans have been given the opportunity to verbally (and / or in writing) express their thoughts, share them with other people. Simply put, we learned how to conduct informed conversations. In our time, communicating with someone for a person is an integral part of his life. Lock a person in the room alone, without communication with the outside world, and you will immediately see the negative results of the lack of the opportunity to talk with someone.







For a normal conversation a person needs a person. Or not? Can the machine (program) replace the live companion? Will the conversation with the woodcutter be as complete and interesting? And is there a future for the interlocutors? We will try to find answers to these and some other questions today.



Historical retreat



Back in 1966, Joseph Weizenbaum, an American scientist and researcher of AI (artificial intelligence), created Eliza, an interlocutor, parodying a psychotherapist.







Weizenbaum decided to use a model of patient and psychotherapist communication because of the complexity of this format of communication. The fact is that in such a conversation, themes and situations are mentioned and described, in which not one word, but several at once can have special meaning. And this is at odds with the program features. Eliza, analyzing the interlocutor's phrase, singled out one root word and gave the answer, starting from it. At that time, the semantic load fell on the whole phrase or a few words. For example, a person says: ‚ÄúI am tired of senseless disputes,‚Äù and here Eliza concentrates on the word tired, as if supposedly basically giving the answer: ‚ÄúWhy are you tired?‚Äù. As you see, a real psychotherapist would consider the entire phrase entirely, analyzing not only why the patient is tired, but what he means by meaningless disputes, when and with whom they had a place to be, etc. A person has a lot of answers for one phrase (or questions), each of which is a consequence of concentration of analytical attention on a particular word in a phrase. Also an important element of communication is the possibility of its continuation, development. Eliza often could not perceive a phrase or highlight the main word, therefore she simply answered ‚ÄúI see‚Äù (Clearly, clearly, I understand - translation) and changed the subject of conversation. Therefore, a person communicating with this program, after a few replicas, could completely get rid of the illusion of communication with a living person.



But Joseph Weizenbaum was a pioneer in the field of human communication and the program. And Eliza was the firstborn of this field of research, so it was impossible to expect a lot from her. Now the computing power of modern computers can significantly expand the capabilities of interlocutors. But where is their edge? Is it possible to 100% replace a person as an interlocutor?



It would be very interesting to read the correspondence of Eliza (virtual psychoanalyst) and another program Parry. And all because its creator, psychiatrist Kenneth Kolby, endowed her with the properties of a person with paranoid schizophrenia. At the same time, Parry was a bit more advanced than its predecessor, since an interactive algorithm was introduced into its program, which allowed to conduct more complex and lengthy negotiations. In 1972, there was a meeting of two programs that talked with each other through ARPANET.
