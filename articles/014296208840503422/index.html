Artichoke: benefits and harms, calories and properties, how to grow and propagate

In some countries, a plant such as an artichoke is very popular among the population. Its benefits and harms have been known since ancient times. This herbaceous plant belongs to the Asteraceae, as it has large edible inflorescences. In fact, this product looks like a huge open bud. The artichoke gained particular fame in the United States, where it was once brought by immigrants from Europe.





The Importance of Artichoke Inflorescences in Cooking

It was in this country that the plant found its deserved destination. Many recipes of national cuisine use not only young fruits, but also mature late kidneys, more reminiscent of old bumps. The first, as a rule, are required for the preparation of appetizers, salads. As for the larger kidneys, they are sent to hot dishes. Cooking options for this plant can not be counted. Indeed, in addition to the indicated methods, the artichoke, the health benefits and harms of which should be familiar to everyone, can be preserved, boiled, and eaten raw. Since only the crushed core of this plant, somewhat reminiscent of a walnut, is suitable for food, wonderful sauces, pastas and mashed potatoes are prepared from it.

















The most important thing is to choose the right fruits. On high-quality artichokes there will never be specks, darkening. Healthy kidneys have a smooth green color, fairly elastic and not too dry. Moreover, the size of such a delicacy absolutely does not matter.









However, the blue flowers of the artichoke, which have blossomed to the end, are not consumed, but they find themselves in the culinary decor.

Beneficial effects on the body

This dietary product can rightfully replace starch, therefore, its use is often recommended for patients with diabetes mellitus. Contraindications to the use of artichoke

Meanwhile, despite a wide range of advantages, the artichoke, the benefits and harms of which should be reasonably balanced before use, has an extremely negative effect on the gastrointestinal tract of people suffering from gastritis, low acidity, and low blood pressure. It is known that the composition of the plant includes the substance polyphenol, which increases the formation and excretion of bile. Therefore, people with cholecystitis, it is better to completely exclude the use of this product from their diet.





The size of the vegetable and the rules for its storage determine the necessary method of preparation. For example, a young bud of inflorescence tastes good even without heat treatment, while a large ripe fruit cannot be eaten raw, since the fibers of such a plant are stiff and tasteless. An artichoke with open brown leaves is generally not suitable for consumption.

Seed preparation

Considering that the artichoke belongs to the vegetable class of plants, the seedling method is excellent for its breeding and cultivation.





The first step in growing is seed preparation. It is advisable to carry out this procedure in early March. To do this, you need a container of water. Seeds should be laid out in water and expect subsequent swelling and germination.





It is important to maintain the required temperature to achieve the desired results. At home, batteries are a constant source of heat at this time of year. However, after the appearance of tiny roots, it is advisable to accustom the seeds to hardening. This can be done by setting the container with sprouts in the cold for 10-14 days.





Landing

Then, seasoned future artichokes (the benefits and harms of growing and preparing a plant - all this information is known to many lovers of this product) are sown in a prepared pot or large box. Before the first transplant from the total capacity to a separate dish, about a month should pass. By the end of spring, ready-made seedlings can be planted in open soil. It is at this point that the likelihood of frost, which can have a detrimental effect on the state of seedlings, is minimal. In the process of planting young plants, it should be remembered that the adult artichoke is large, so it is best to distribute young seedlings at a distance of 60-70 cm from each other at a depth of 20-25 cm.





How to care for artichoke?

Caring for this plant, as well as for many other vegetable crops, does not require a special approach. He also needs loose soil, the absence of nearby weeds, and the implementation of preventive measures aimed at preventing diseases and parasites. An artichoke, the benefits and harms of which will not leave anyone indifferent, does not need frequent heavy watering. Of course, in case of drought, it is desirable to provide the plant with sufficient moisture.













You can get rid of slugs and black rot by spraying with special solutions. Basically resort to the use of infusions of volatile. Useful insects (bees, wasps, etc.) will warn the artichoke against aphids, therefore, nectar-bearing crops or flowers are often planted next to this vegetable.
